Frequently Asked Questions

General

What are the functionalities of pedigrees and Punnett squares?
These two visual concepts are an integral part of most biology courses taught from the secondary school level and up, and gaining an understanding of how genetics works through a visual aid can be a boon for most teachers. In addition, pedigrees and Punnett squares are an effective tool for users to determine likelihood of diseases being passed from parent to child.

Pedigrees focus on displaying the relationships within a family, and are effective to look at genetic inheritance on a "macro" scale.

Punnett squares focus on displaying possible offspring that may be produced when two parents have a child, and are effective to look at genetic inheritance on a "micro" scale.

Pedigree

What modes of inheritance are supported?
The Linnaeus Pedigree supports the most common mode of inheritance, autosomal recessive.
Why is knowing the carrier probability significant?
A carrier does not exhibit the recessive phenotype, but he/she carries the recessive allele. An offspring of two carriers is thus at risk of inheriting two recessive alleles and exhibiting the diseased phenotype.

By knowing the probability of an individual being a carrier, informed conclusions can be drawn regarding the likelihood of his/her children being afflicted with a disease.
How do I calculate the probability of two parents having an infected child in the future?
Using the pedigree, you can determine the most likely genotype of each parent (a purely unaffected parent has genotype AA; a carrier parent has genotype Aa; an infected parent has genotype aa). You may then utilize these genotypes in the Punnett square function to determine the possible offspring genotypes and phenotypes.

Punnett Square

What are the different Punnett square sizes?
The Linnaeus Punnett square supports three sizes: 2x2, 4x4, 8x8, and 16x16. Each size determines the possible offspring genotypes and phenotypes for a different number of traits.

2x2: One trait
4x4: Two traits
8x8: Three traits
16x16: Four traits
What do AA, Aa, and aa mean?
A parent genotype can take one of three forms: homozygous dominant (AA), heterozygous (Aa), or homozygous recessive (aa). The corresponding phenotype can be determined upon deciding the mode of inheritance.
What modes of inheritance are supported?
The Linnaeus Punnett square supports three modes of inheritance: autosomal dominant, codominance, and incomplete dominance.
How are these modes of inheritance different?
Autosomal: The dominant phenotype only will be expressed as long as at least one dominant allele is inherited (genotype AA or Aa). This is represented with a fully shaded cell.

Codominance: If both the dominant allele and the recessive allele are present (genotype Aa), both the dominant and the recessive phenotypes will be expressed. This is represented with a half-shaded cell.

Incomplete Dominance: If both the dominant allele and the recessive allele are present (genotype Aa), the phenotype will be an intermediate of dominant and recessive. This is represented with a lightly shaded cell.
